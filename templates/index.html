Welcome to Grorg.

This is the semi-experimental system to help manage grant applications, originally developed by Andrew Godwin. This is a customised version with extra options added for DjangoCon Europe.

DjangoCon Europe 2018 review process

To become a reviewer, ask Sasha for an account. Once you are logged in, under "Applicants" you can see all applicants. Click "score" to read and score an application, and then "save & next" to see the next one.

You can score an application from 1 (really bad) to 5 (really good), and optionally add a comment about something that caught your eye in a good or bad way. Your comments should be pretty brief - if we need to look at an application in more detail, we'll ask you for more explanation later. Scores can have decimals (e.g. 4.5). Please ensure that you score all applications eventually, but you don't have to do it in a single sitting.

You'll also find answers about underrepresented groups, visa requirements and whether they applied for the CFP. Do not take these particular answers into account in your score. Some of this information still needs to be updated for the CFP results, and we will later have a separate mechanism for prioritising those answers correctly.

The applicant's name and email are hidden until you have assigned a first score, to reduce bias. You can see them after you've assigned a score. You can still change your score, but a note will be added with your original score.

Our goal is to maximise the impact our grant program has, within the limited funds we have. The score you assign should be based on the overall impact that granting this person will have on them and on their wider community and country, compared to others, and taking into account the financial impact. Some basic guidelines:

* To what extent will letting this person attend have a further impact on our community? For example, someone who essentially wrote “I will help my company make more money” should probably be scored lower than someone who is active in their local community.

* Social impact can be a part too: we should score “I use Django to help this bank I work for become richer” lower than “I work with Django as part of a project to help addicted homeless people towards recovery”.

* Where is this person from? If they would take a large budget, but live in an area that has many other conferences closer by, that may be a better choice. So all other things being equal, we would rather spend € 1000 on an attendee from Kenia than from Portland, Oregon, US.

* How reasonable is the cost? We're unlikely to spend € 10.000 on a single applicant even if they have an amazing story. On the other hand, if someone only wants a free ticket and no money because they live nearby, the bar isn't so high. Cost is not the prime consideration though, because otherwise we'd only ever select people that live near, and some people that we specifically want to include simply have higher travel costs than others.

* Are they making a basic effort? For example, if someone won’t even have an estimate of the price of their flight without further explanation, I’m assuming they couldn’t be bothered to search.
